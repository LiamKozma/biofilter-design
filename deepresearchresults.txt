The Monod equation defines the specific microbial growth or substrate utilization rate as a hyperbolic function of the local substrate concentration in the liquid or biofilm phase. However, structural identifiability analyses based on Fisher Information Theory demonstrate that when the local substrate concentration is significantly lower than the half-saturation constant, the denominator of the Monod expression is dominated by the half-saturation constant. Practitioners in biofiltration engineering commonly encounter this "over-performing bed" problem when utilizing highly active media, such as proprietary biosorbents mixed with compost, at extended empty-bed contact times. To derive precise intrinsic kinetic parameters, the biofilter must be operated in a reaction-limited regime rather than a starvation-limited regime. Historically, models of the Ottengraf-Van Den Oever type assumed either zero-order kinetics—operating in diffusion-limited or reaction-limited regimes—or first-order kinetics to derive simple analytical expressions for concentration profiles. The implementation of a fully numerical Monod-based two-scale model with Danckwerts boundary conditions is physically superior for capturing transitional behavior, provided the experimental data can support the identification of the underlying parameters.However, the assumption of Danckwerts boundary conditions and the inclusion of significant axial dispersion may be unnecessarily complex for a highly efficient packed bed treating low concentrations of volatile organic compounds. In typical compost and synthetic-media biofilters, the Peclet number, which represents the ratio of the rate of advection to the rate of axial diffusion, ranges from 100 to 600. When the Peclet number exceeds 100, the macroscopic concentration profile becomes essentially independent of axial dispersion, and the system behaves mathematically as an ideal plug-flow reactor. The axial dispersion parameter is likely contributing to the computational burden of the Bayesian inference without providing any explanatory power. This aligns with the global Sobol sensitivity analysis, which indicated that axial dispersion had a negligible effect on the targeted design output.Effectiveness Factor and Thiele Modulus ApproximationsSolving the secondary boundary value problem for the biofilm effectiveness factor numerically at every spatial node and during every Markov Chain Monte Carlo step introduces extreme computational stiffness and dramatically slows down the sampling algorithm. Furthermore, it obscures the parametric dependencies that drive the structural unidentifiability. The Thiele modulus for Monod kinetics in a flat slab geometry determines the transition between reaction-limited and diffusion-limited regimes.When the bulk concentration is heavily depleted, as it is in the latter two-thirds of the experimental reactor, the generalized Thiele modulus simplifies to the first-order Thiele modulus. In the diffusion-limited asymptote, the effectiveness factor is inversely proportional to the Thiele modulus. If the system enters this regime, substituting the asymptotic effectiveness factor into the lumped apparent rate constant demonstrates that the biofilm thickness cancels out entirely. The physical interpretation is that the volatile organic compounds degrade completely within the outermost boundary of the biofilm; the deeper layers are starved and inactive. Consequently, the column data contains absolutely no information regarding the true biofilm thickness.Validated algebraic approximations for the effectiveness factor under generic non-linear kinetics provide highly accurate pseudo-analytical solutions that can completely replace the nested boundary value problem. For example, the Gottifredi and Gonzo approximations utilizing perturbation and matching techniques have been proven to estimate the effectiveness factor across the entire range of the Thiele modulus with maximum deviations of less than two percent.
